Introduction to Retailing

Definition

Retailing refers to all the activities involved in selling goods or services directly to the final consumer for personal, non-business use. It includes every sale to the final consumer — regardless of how the goods or services are sold (in-store, by mail, telephone, or online).

Philip Kotler defines retailing as:

"Retailing includes all the activities involved in selling goods or services directly to final consumers for personal, non-business use."

Nature and Scope of Retailing

Nature:

  • Direct interaction with customers.
  • Involves small quantities of products.
  • Customer service is a critical element.
  • Highly competitive sector.
  • Constant adaptation to consumer behavior.

Scope:

  • Encompasses a wide range of industries — clothing, food, electronics, furniture, etc.
  • Covers different selling formats — physical stores, kiosks, e-commerce platforms, vending machines, etc.
  • Involves aspects like supply chain management, merchandising, store layout, marketing, and branding.

Evolution of Retailing

  • Traditional Markets: Local markets and fairs where producers directly sold goods.
  • Mom-and-Pop Stores: Small family-owned stores in neighborhoods.
  • Department Stores: Large stores offering a variety of goods under one roof (e.g., Macy’s).
  • Chain Stores: Retail companies with multiple outlets under the same brand.
  • Supermarkets and Hypermarkets: Large self-service stores (e.g., Walmart).
  • E-commerce: Online platforms (e.g., Amazon, Flipkart) revolutionized retailing.
  • Omnichannel Retailing: Integration of offline and online experiences for customers.

Types of Retail

By Ownership:

  • Independent Retailer
  • Corporate Chain
  • Franchise
  • Cooperative

By Store-based Retailing:

  • Department Stores
  • Supermarkets
  • Specialty Stores
  • Convenience Stores
  • Discount Stores
  • Hypermarkets

By Non-store Retailing:

  • E-commerce
  • Direct Selling
  • Telemarketing
  • Vending Machines
  • Home Shopping Networks

Trends in the Retailing Industry

  • Technology Integration: Use of AI, AR/VR, data analytics.
  • Omnichannel Retailing: Seamless shopping across online and offline.
  • Sustainability: Eco-friendly practices gaining traction.
  • Personalized Experience: Tailoring offers and communication to individuals.
  • Social Commerce: Selling through platforms like Instagram, Facebook.
  • Rise of Experiential Retail: Creating engaging in-store experiences.
  • Quick Commerce: Delivery within minutes (e.g., Blinkit in India).

Benefits of Retailing

  • Convenience for Consumers: Access to a variety of products at nearby locations or online.
  • Job Creation: Employment opportunities in sales, logistics, marketing, and management.
  • Economic Growth: Contributes to GDP, taxes, and economic development.
  • Facilitates Market Reach: Helps manufacturers reach a wide customer base.
  • Enhances Consumer Choices: Competition drives quality and innovation.

Retailing Environment in India

  • Vibrant and Growing: One of the world’s fastest-growing retail markets.
  • Diverse Formats: Mix of traditional Kirana stores and modern retail outlets.
  • Favorable Demographics: Large young population and growing middle class.
  • Government Initiatives: FDI policies in retail, 'Make in India,' 'Digital India' drive sector growth.
  • E-commerce Boom: Major players like Flipkart, Amazon, Reliance Retail dominating.
  • Challenges: Infrastructure issues, regulatory complexities, and intense competition.
  • Opportunities: Untapped rural markets, demand for luxury and premium products, innovation in payment systems (e.g., UPI).

Concept & Importance of Single Brand Retail

Concept of Single Brand Retail

Single Brand Retail refers to retailing where a business sells goods under one brand name. The retailer either produces the goods themselves or is closely associated with the brand's identity.

Definition:

Single Brand Retail means a business selling products from a single label to consumers, such as Nike, Adidas, Zara, or Apple, through exclusive stores.

Importance:

  • Brand Control: Companies maintain full control over brand image and customer experience.
  • Customer Loyalty: Strong brand identity builds loyal customer bases.
  • Global Expansion: Easier for international brands to enter new markets.
  • Quality Assurance: Customers get standardized product quality and service.

Concept and Definition of Multi Brand Retail

Concept of Multi Brand Retail

Multi Brand Retail refers to stores that sell products of multiple brands under one roof.

Definition:

Multi Brand Retail involves selling goods from several brands, often competitors, through a single retail outlet — like Big Bazaar, Reliance Retail, Shoppers Stop, and Walmart.

Examples:

  • Department stores (e.g., Lifestyle)
  • Hypermarkets (e.g., Walmart)
  • Online marketplaces (e.g., Amazon, Flipkart)

History of FDI in Single Brand Retail in India

  • Pre-2006: FDI in retail was restricted. No foreign investment allowed.
  • 2006: India allowed 51% FDI in Single Brand Retail Trading (SBRT) under approval route (government permission needed).
  • 2012: FDI limit increased to 100% for SBRT, but government approval was still required for investment beyond 51%.

2018:

  • 100% FDI allowed under automatic route (no need for government approval) if certain conditions were met.
  • Brands must sell products under the same brand internationally.
  • 30% of sourcing must be done from Indian small industries if FDI exceeds 51%.

History of FDI in Multi Brand Retail in India

Before 2012: FDI in multi brand retail was completely prohibited.

2012:

  • India allowed up to 51% FDI in Multi Brand Retail Trading (MBRT) with government approval.
  • Subject to strict conditions like:
  • Minimum investment of $100 million.
  • 50% of investment must be in backend infrastructure (warehouses, logistics).
  • 30% local sourcing from small and medium enterprises.
  • Retail outlets allowed only in cities with population over 1 million (initially).

Current Status: FDI in multi brand retail remains capped at 51% and requires government approval. It is a state-specific decision (some states have allowed it, others have not).

Pros and Cons of Allowing FDI in Single and Multi Brand Retail

Pros:

For Both Single and Multi Brand:

  • Brings in foreign investment, boosting the economy.
  • Enhances competition, leading to better quality and lower prices.
  • Generates employment opportunities across the supply chain.
  • Promotes infrastructure development (storage, transport, logistics).

Specific to Single Brand:

  • Helps global brands establish exclusive customer experiences.
  • Improves local sourcing and technology transfer.

Specific to Multi Brand:

  • Modernizes the retail sector.
  • Benefits farmers and small producers by improving supply chain efficiency.

Cons:

For Both:

  • Fear of domination by foreign players affecting local businesses.
  • Risk to small retailers (Kirana stores).
  • May lead to urban-centric development, neglecting rural markets.

Specific to Single Brand:

  • Brands may struggle with 30% mandatory local sourcing requirements.

Specific to Multi Brand:

  • Possible monopolization by large retail chains.
  • Complexity due to state-level restrictions.

Retail Marketing and Promotion

1. Relationship Marketing

Relationship marketing focuses on building long-term relationships with customers rather than just making one-time sales.

Key Features:

  • Personalization of services.
  • Customer loyalty programs.
  • Regular communication (emails, SMS updates).
  • Building trust and engagement.

Importance: In retail, relationship marketing leads to repeat business, word-of-mouth referrals, and brand loyalty.

2. Market Strategies

Retailers use different marketing strategies to reach and serve customers:

  • Mass Marketing: Targeting a wide audience (e.g., Big Bazaar).
  • Niche Marketing: Serving a specific segment (e.g., Fabindia for ethnic wear).
  • Differentiation Strategy: Offering unique products/services.
  • Cost Leadership: Competing on low prices (e.g., D-Mart).
  • Omnichannel Strategy: Integrating physical stores, online platforms, and mobile apps for a seamless experience.

3. Retail Research

Retail research involves collecting and analyzing information to make better business decisions.

Types:

  • Market Research: Understanding market size, trends, and competition.
  • Customer Research: Studying buying behavior, preferences.
  • Location Research: Selecting store locations based on footfall, demographics.
  • Product Research: Understanding which products are in demand.

4. Understanding Retail Customers

Retailers must understand:

  • Needs and Wants: What problems customers are trying to solve.
  • Buying Motivations: Price, convenience, quality, brand image.
  • Decision-Making Process: How customers decide what and where to buy.
  • Customer Expectations: Service levels, shopping experience, after-sales support.

5. Population and Demographic Analysis

Retailers study demographics to tailor their products and services.

Key Demographic Factors:

  • Age
  • Gender
  • Income Level
  • Education
  • Occupation
  • Lifestyle Patterns

Example: A youth-focused store will open outlets near colleges or urban centers.

6. Consumer Behaviour

Consumer behavior studies how individuals select, buy, use, and dispose of goods.

Influencing Factors:

  • Cultural: Traditions, beliefs.
  • Social: Family, friends, reference groups.
  • Personal: Age, income, lifestyle.
  • Psychological: Motivation, perception, attitude.

Buying Decision Process:

  1. Problem Recognition
  2. Information Search
  3. Evaluation of Alternatives
  4. Purchase Decision
  5. Post-Purchase Behavior

7. Retail Promotion Mix

The tools retailers use to communicate with customers:

  • Advertising: Paid, non-personal communication (TV ads, online ads).
  • Sales Promotion: Discounts, coupons, loyalty points.
  • Public Relations (PR): Publicity, CSR activities.
  • Personal Selling: Staff interactions with customers.
  • Direct Marketing: Emails, SMS, catalogues.

8. Advertising Media

Retailers choose media based on target customers:

  • Traditional Media: Newspapers, magazines, radio, TV.
  • Digital Media: Social media, websites, email marketing, apps.
  • Outdoor Media: Billboards, transit ads (buses, trains).
  • In-Store Media: Posters, digital screens, product displays inside the store.

9. Promotional Budget

Deciding how much to spend on promotions:

  • Methods:
  • Percentage of Sales Method: Budget = % of expected sales.
  • Competitive Parity: Matching competitor spending.
  • Objective and Task Method: Budget based on specific goals.
  • Affordable Method: Based on what the retailer can afford.

A good promotional budget balances visibility and cost control.

10. Customer Services

Includes all services provided before, during, and after a purchase:

  • Product guidance
  • Easy return policies
  • After-sales service
  • Loyalty programs
  • Handling complaints

Good customer service is critical to customer satisfaction and retention.

11. Service Quality Gaps

The Service Quality Gap Model identifies differences between customer expectations and retailer performance.

Key Gaps:

  • Knowledge Gap: Retailer doesn’t know what customers expect.
  • Policy Gap: Wrong service standards.
  • Delivery Gap: Poor service execution.
  • Communication Gap: Promises in marketing don't match reality.
  • Customer Gap: Overall difference between expectations and experiences.

Closing these gaps leads to higher customer satisfaction.

12. Service Recovery

What a retailer does when service fails.

Effective service recovery includes:

  • Acknowledging the problem
  • Apologizing sincerely
  • Fixing the issue promptly
  • Offering compensation (if needed)
  • Following up to ensure customer satisfaction

Good service recovery can turn an unhappy customer into a loyal one.

Information System in Retailing

Retailers need accurate, timely information to:

Information acquisition strategies include:

Using information effectively means:

  • Turning raw data into actionable insights.
  • Improving decision-making across departments (sales, marketing, logistics, HR).
  • Gaining competitive advantage.

Retailers gather information from various sources:

  • Customer databases
  • Inventory reports
  • Employee feedback
  • External Sources:
  • Market research firms (e.g., Nielsen)
  • Competitor analysis
  • Social media trends
  • Government publications and industry reports

Retailers combine internal and external sources for a full view of the market and customer needs.

4. Retail Information System (RIS)

Definition:

A Retail Information System is a structured setup that collects, stores, and processes retail data to support decision-making.

Components of RIS:

  • Transaction Processing Systems (TPS): Manage sales transactions (POS systems).
  • Management Information Systems (MIS): Analyze and summarize transaction data for managers.
  • Decision Support Systems (DSS): Help in making strategic decisions (like opening a new store).
  • Customer Relationship Management (CRM) Systems: Manage detailed customer information.
  • Inventory Management Systems: Keep track of stock levels, automate reordering.

Benefits of RIS:

  • Faster decision-making.
  • Improved customer service.
  • Optimized inventory control.
  • Better financial management.

Retailing in India

1. Evolution and Trends in Organized Retailing

Evolution:

  • Pre-1990s: Traditional retail (Kirana stores, weekly markets, local bazaars) dominated.
  • 1990s (Post-Liberalization): Economic reforms led to the entry of private players like Shoppers Stop, Pantaloons.
  • 2000s: Growth of malls, supermarkets, specialty stores; entry of international brands.
  • 2010s onward:
  • Rise of e-commerce (Flipkart, Amazon).
  • Growth of luxury and premium retail.
  • Omnichannel presence became important (combination of physical + online stores).
  • Quick commerce (e.g., Blinkit) gaining popularity.

Key Trends:

  • Growth of private labels (in-house brands).
  • Focus on customer experience (personalization, loyalty programs).
  • Use of AI, Big Data, and Analytics.
  • Expansion into tier II and tier III cities.
  • Sustainability and ethical sourcing.

2. Indian Organized Retail Market

Definition:

Organized retail refers to trading activities undertaken by licensed retailers (corporate-backed stores, franchise chains, etc.) as opposed to unorganized retail (Kirana shops, local vendors).

Market Features:

  • Still developing — organized retail accounts for about 12–15% of the total retail market (expected to grow significantly).
  • Highly diverse — fashion, food & grocery, electronics, furniture, etc.
  • Major Players: Reliance Retail, Tata Group (Trent, Croma), Future Group (Big Bazaar), Aditya Birla Group, D-Mart.

Growth Drivers:

  • Urbanization and rising disposable incomes.
  • Increasing internet penetration.
  • Changing consumer preferences toward branded goods.

3. FDI in Indian Organized Retail Sector

FDI Policy:

  • Single Brand Retail:
  • 100% FDI allowed (automatic route up to 100% with local sourcing norms).
  • Multi Brand Retail:
  • 51% FDI allowed with government approval.
  • Conditions: minimum $100 million investment, 50% in backend infrastructure, 30% local sourcing from SMEs.

Impact of FDI:

  • Increased competition, bringing better quality and global brands.
  • Boost to employment, infrastructure, and supply chains.
  • Better technology and management practices.

Challenges:

  • Political resistance from small retailer lobbies.
  • State-specific permissions required for multi-brand retail.

4. Retail Scenario in India

  • Large and Fragmented: Mostly small and family-run stores.
  • Emergence of Organized Retail: Fast growth in urban areas.
  • E-commerce boom: Especially after COVID-19 pandemic.
  • Omnichannel Retailing: Integration of online, mobile, and physical stores.
  • Sector Mix:
  • Food and Grocery: Largest segment.
  • Apparel, Electronics, Home Décor, Health & Wellness sectors are growing rapidly.

5. Future Trends

  • Growth of phygital experiences (physical + digital stores).
  • Increased use of AI, AR/VR to personalize customer experience.
  • Rapid penetration of tier II and III cities.
  • Focus on sustainability (eco-friendly packaging, energy-efficient stores).
  • Private Labels gaining strength to boost margins.
  • Subscription-based models (e.g., Amazon Prime).
  • Rise of experiential retail (the store becomes an experience center, not just a place to buy).

6. Ethical and Legal Issues

Ethical Issues:

  • Misleading advertisements.
  • Fair pricing practices.
  • Non-disclosure of product information.
  • Use of consumer data (privacy concerns).

Legal Issues:

  • Compliance with Consumer Protection Act.
  • Retail-specific laws:
  • Legal Metrology Act (packaging and labeling).
  • FDI regulations.
  • GST compliance.
  • E-commerce rules: Listing transparency, consumer grievance redressal.

Retailers must follow ethical practices and legal frameworks to build long-term trust.

7. Social Responsibility in Retail

Retailers are increasingly taking up Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) activities:

  • Promoting ethical sourcing (fair trade).
  • Supporting local artisans and small businesses.
  • Inclusive employment (hiring from disadvantaged groups).
  • Community welfare programs (education, health initiatives).

Example: Tanishq supports traditional jewelry artisans through skill development.

8. Environmental Orientation

Retailers are adopting green practices to minimize their environmental impact:

  • Eco-friendly store designs (LED lighting, green buildings).
  • Sustainable sourcing of raw materials.
  • Promoting reusable bags instead of plastic.
  • Energy-efficient supply chains.

9. Waste Reduction at Retail Stores

Strategies retailers use to minimize waste:

  • Inventory Management: Prevent overstocking and spoilage.
  • Recycling Programs: Collection of used products for recycling.
  • Zero-Waste Stores: Initiatives where stores aim to send zero waste to landfills.
  • Composting: Food retailers compost organic waste.
  • Donation of Unsold Goods: Donating rather than destroying unsold inventory.

Example:

Big Bazaar’s "Green Bazaar" initiative encourages eco-friendly practices.
